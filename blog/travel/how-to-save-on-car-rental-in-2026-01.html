How to Save Money on Car Rentals in 2026:
11 Proven Ways to Cut Costs

Updated June 2026 · Maison Sarddinnah Editorial Team · 15 min read
Home › Travel › How to Save on Car Rental in 2026

Car rental has become one of those travel costs that quietly balloons your budget — often more than the flight itself. I learned this the hard way on a trip to Portugal a few years ago, what should have been a €150 rental somehow turned into €340 by the time I returned the keys. The fees were everywhere: airport surcharges, insurance I didn't need, fuel policies I didn't understand, and add-ons I declined but somehow still appeared on the final bill.

In 2026, with dynamic pricing, surprise fees, and a dozen platforms competing for your click, knowing how to rent a car is just as important as knowing where you're going. The rental industry has evolved — they've gotten smarter at extracting money, but you can get smarter at keeping it.

Whether you're picking up a compact in Lisbon, cruising coastal Japan in a kei car, or navigating the wide open roads of Iceland, the rules of smart car rental are universal — and in 2026, they've evolved. Rental companies are smarter. So are the comparison tools. And so, after this guide, will you be.

Below, we break down every layer of the car rental game: when to book, where to compare, what to decline at the counter, and the sneaky fees that cost travellers hundreds they never expected to spend.

I've spent years testing these strategies across three continents, from the winding roads of Tuscany to the highways of California and the mountain passes of New Zealand. What I've learned is that saving money on car rentals isn't about finding one magic discount — it's about understanding the entire system and making smart decisions at every stage of the process. These aren't theoretical tips — they're strategies I use personally, and they've saved me thousands over the years.

1. Book Early — But Keep Rebooking

The single most effective thing you can do to save money on a car rental is also the most counterintuitive: book early, then rebook if prices drop. Unlike flights, most reputable rental platforms allow free cancellation up to 24–48 hours before pickup. This means locking in a rate early protects you against price surges — but you're not stuck with it.

According to data published by major travel industry sources and rental booking platforms like Rentalcars.com research, booking several weeks in advance remains one of the most effective ways to secure lower rates and better vehicle availability.

Here's how this works in practice: Let's say you're planning a trip to Sicily for July. You book a compact car in February for €25 per day. By May, the same car is showing at €35 per day — you're already saving. But then in June, prices spike to €45 per day due to high demand. Because you booked early with free cancellation, you're locked in at that €25 rate. If prices had dropped instead, you could have cancelled and rebooked at the lower rate. It's a win-win strategy.

Set a reminder to check your rental price weekly once you've booked. If a cheaper rate appears for the same vehicle category and dates, cancel and rebook. Think of it as auto-negotiating against yourself.

Pro Tip

Book the moment you confirm your travel dates, even if your plans feel tentative. Rates on popular destinations like Santorini, Bali, or the Azores can triple in the weeks before peak season. A free-cancellation booking costs you nothing but a few minutes.

I learned this lesson the hard way when planning a trip to the Greek islands. I waited until three weeks before departure to book a car, thinking I was being organized. What I found was that most reputable suppliers were completely sold out, and the few remaining options were charging triple what I would have paid if I'd booked two months earlier. I ended up taking a bus and ferry combination that cost more in time and stress than the rental would have.

In 2026, inventory shortages at major airports during summer and holiday windows remain a real issue. Beyond the price benefit, booking early ensures you have a car at all — not just a better rate on one.

2. Use a Comparison Platform Before Booking Direct

Never start your search on a rental company’s website. Instead, always begin with a comparison platform — it takes under two minutes and can significantly reduce your total cost by surfacing competing offers from multiple suppliers.

Modern car rental pricing is highly dynamic. The same vehicle can vary in price depending on demand, location, and supplier margins. Comparison platforms solve this by aggregating multiple providers in one place, giving you access to rates that are often not visible on direct booking sites.

I used to make the mistake of going directly to Hertz or Avis websites, thinking I was getting the best deal by booking direct. Then I discovered that the same car on the same dates could be 30-40% cheaper through a comparison platform that aggregates multiple suppliers. The rental companies don't advertise this, of course — they'd rather you pay their direct rates.

Instead of relying on generic market reports, real-world booking data consistently shows that travellers who compare across multiple suppliers secure better overall pricing and more flexible terms than those who book directly with a single brand.

Our recommended comparison tools are Localrent (via TravelPayouts), which focuses on trusted local suppliers alongside major brands and often reveals rates 30–50% lower than standard international rental companies. It is particularly strong in destinations such as Southern Europe, the Balkans, and parts of Asia where local fleets dominate pricing.

For broader international coverage, GetRentacar provides transparent global pricing across multiple suppliers, allowing you to compare offers side-by-side before committing. It is especially useful for long-distance trips or multi-country itineraries where availability and cross-border rules vary.

On a recent trip through Croatia and Slovenia, I used GetRentacar to compare cross-border rental options. What I found was that some suppliers charged astronomical fees for taking a car from Croatia into Slovenia, while others included it at no extra cost. Without that comparison, I would have unknowingly paid an extra €150 in cross-border fees.

For additional comparison flexibility, you can also explore platforms like QEEQ or EconomyBookings, which expand coverage across smaller regional suppliers and can occasionally surface niche deals not listed on larger platforms.

Pro Tip: Always sort results by total price including fees, not the daily rate. Many listings appear cheaper upfront but add mandatory insurance, young driver fees, or location surcharges at checkout.

This is where most people get caught. I once booked what seemed like an incredible €15 per day deal in Barcelona, only to discover at checkout that mandatory insurance and young driver fees brought the actual daily cost to €42. Had I sorted by total price from the beginning, I would have seen a different supplier offering €28 per day all-in — a genuine better deal.

The car rental industry runs on the gap between what's advertised and what's charged. Your job is to close that gap before you reach the counter.

3. Choose the Right Vehicle Category

Rental pricing is largely driven by vehicle category, and most travellers instinctively book too large. A full-size SUV might feel comfortable, but it costs more to rent, more to fuel, and — in European cities especially — more to park.

I've seen this pattern countless times. Friends book an SUV for a week in Tuscany "for the space," then spend half the trip stressed about narrow streets, tight parking spots, and fuel costs that double their budget. Meanwhile, I'm cruising comfortably in a compact that fits everywhere and costs half as much to run.

In 2026, the practical logic for vehicle selection looks like this:

Trip Type Recommended Category Why
City weekend, 1–2 people Mini / Economy Easiest to park, cheapest to fuel, widely available
Road trip, 2–4 people with luggage Compact / Intermediate Balance of comfort, fuel economy, and boot space
Family or group travel, 5+ Estate / Minivan More economical than two cars; check 7-seater rates
Coastal / scenic drives Compact Convertible Often less expensive than full-size SUVs; experience first
Off-road or mountain terrain 4WD / SUV Only if the terrain genuinely requires it — Iceland, rural Morocco, etc.
Category Upgrade Trick

Book the smallest category that could work for you, then ask at the counter if a free upgrade is available. Rental desks sometimes offer voluntary upgrades when higher categories are oversupplied — at no cost to you. This works best midweek at non-airport locations.

This strategy has worked for me more times than I can count. On a trip to Portugal, I booked an economy car but was upgraded to a compact SUV at no extra cost because the economy fleet was oversubscribed. The key is to be polite, flexible, and ask nicely — sometimes the counter staff have discretion they're willing to use for friendly customers.

4. Never Buy Rental Company Insurance (Usually)

This is where rental companies make a disproportionate share of their money. At the counter, you'll be offered a Collision Damage Waiver (CDW), Super CDW, Personal Accident Insurance, and often a Tire and Windscreen package. Together, these can double the cost of a booking.

Before accepting anything, check these three sources first:

Your credit card

Many premium Visa and Mastercard credit cards include complimentary car rental insurance when you pay for the rental with that card. Coverage details vary — most cover collision and theft but not personal accident or third-party liability. Call your card provider before your trip to confirm what's included and what documentation you'd need to make a claim.

I discovered this by accident when my Chase Sapphire card saved me over €200 on a rental in Italy. The rental company wanted €15 per day for CDW, but my card covered it automatically. The key is to pay for the entire rental with that card and keep the rental agreement — you'll need both if you ever need to make a claim.

Your home travel insurance policy

Annual multi-trip travel insurance policies from most major providers include car rental excess cover as standard or as a low-cost add-on. If you travel more than twice a year, an annual policy that includes rental excess coverage is almost always cheaper than buying cover trip by trip — let alone at the rental counter.

When I switched to an annual travel insurance policy that included rental excess cover, I calculated that I saved over €400 in the first year alone. The policy cost me €180 for the year, but I would have paid €580 for equivalent coverage at rental counters across five different trips. It's one of those travel expenses that pays for itself quickly if you're a frequent traveller.

Standalone rental excess insurance

If neither of the above applies, dedicated car hire excess policies from providers like EKTA Insurance or AirHelp (via Travelpayouts) are typically 60–80% cheaper than what the rental desk charges. Purchase before your trip, not the morning of pickup.

Watch Out

Some rental companies include a mandatory "Basic" CDW in all bookings — this covers the car but leaves a large excess (deductible) that you'd pay if damaged. This isn't optional insurance; it's their standard liability structure. What you're being sold at the counter is protection against that excess. Know the difference before you sign.

This distinction tripped me up on my first few international rentals. I thought I was declining unnecessary insurance, only to realize later that I was still on the hook for a €1,500 excess if anything happened. Now I always read the fine print before booking and understand exactly what's included in the base rate versus what's being offered as an upsell.

5. Pick Up Away From the Airport

Airport rental desks carry a premium — not just from the rental company, but from the airport authority itself, which charges concession fees to operators for the privilege of being in arrivals. Those fees are passed directly to you. On some routes, the same car from the same supplier can be 25–40% cheaper from a city-centre or downtown location.

The first time I experienced this was in Paris. I booked an airport pickup for convenience, only to discover later that the same car from the same company would have cost me €35 per day less if I'd picked it up at their Gare du Nord location. That's €245 over a week — enough to cover several nice dinners. Lesson learned: convenience has a price, and sometimes it's worth taking a taxi or metro to a city location to save significantly.

From Experience

During a two-week road trip through Portugal, we found airport rates in Lisbon were nearly 35% higher than city-centre pickup locations. Waiting until the following morning to collect the vehicle saved enough to cover several days of fuel costs.

The trade-off is logistics: you'll need to get to the pickup location first (metro, taxi, airport bus) and factor in that time. For trips starting immediately after landing, the airport premium may be worth it. But for city breaks where you're not planning to drive on day one, consider picking up the following morning from a downtown office. You'll often pay less, deal with shorter queues, and encounter a more relaxed counter experience.

This approach works particularly well in European cities where public transport from the airport is efficient and affordable. I've taken the RER B train from Charles de Gaulle to central Paris many times, picked up a rental the next morning, and saved hundreds in the process. The key is planning your arrival day around not needing the car immediately.

One-Way Drop Tip

If your itinerary ends at a different city, compare one-way drop fees carefully. Some suppliers charge disproportionately high drop fees; others, particularly local independent suppliers on well-travelled routes, include it at no extra cost. Helpful breakdowns of these cross-region mechanics are regularly catalogued in Auto Europe guides.

6. Fuel Policies — The Full-to-Full Rule

Every rental booking comes with a fuel policy, and the wrong one can quietly cost you a lot. In 2026, the standard options are:

Full-to-Full (recommended)

You pick up with a full tank and return it full. Simple, transparent, no games. This is the only policy that puts the cost of fuel entirely under your control. Always choose this when available.

This is the only fuel policy I ever accept. I learned the hard way with a prepaid fuel policy in Spain — I paid for a full tank upfront at €1.80 per liter (well above market rates), returned the car with half a tank still unused, and essentially donated €40 to the rental company. Never again.

Full-to-Empty / Prepaid Fuel

You prepay for a full tank upfront at the rental company's (inflated) rate, then return it empty. You're guaranteed to pay for more fuel than you use. Avoid unless genuinely driving from one end of the country to the other.

Same-to-Same

Return with approximately the same level as pickup. In practice, this is hard to replicate exactly — and companies can charge refuelling fees if they judge the level to be lower. Risky.

Refuelling Fee Trap

If you return a car needing refuelling under a full-to-full policy, rental companies charge significantly above-market rates for the service — sometimes 3–4× the pump price, plus an admin fee. Fill the tank at a petrol station within the last 10 km before return. Keep the receipt in case of dispute.

I once made the mistake of assuming I could return a car "close enough" to full and it would be fine. The rental company charged me €45 for refuelling when the actual fuel cost would have been €18 at a nearby station. They also added a €15 "admin fee" for the privilege of being overcharged. Now I always fill up within 5 km of the return location and keep the receipt as proof.

7. Use Loyalty Programmes and Membership Discounts

Most major rental chains — Hertz, Enterprise, Avis, Budget, Sixt, Europcar — offer free loyalty programmes that cost nothing to join and provide real benefits: faster pickup, skip-the-counter service, and regular member-rate discounts.

I joined every major rental loyalty programme years ago, and the cumulative savings have been substantial. Beyond the direct discounts, the time saved at the counter is invaluable — especially when you've just landed after a long flight and just want to get on the road. Some programmes even offer guaranteed upgrades for frequent members, which I've received several times without asking.

8. Understand and Avoid Hidden Fees

The headline rental rate is rarely what you pay. In 2026, the most common hidden or unexpected fees that inflate final bills include:

Additional Driver Fee

Adding a second driver at the counter typically costs €10–20 per day. If two of you plan to share driving, factor this in when comparing prices — or look for suppliers that offer free additional drivers (some European independents and many US agencies do during promotions).

Young Driver Surcharge

Drivers under 25 (sometimes under 28) face daily surcharges that can rival the base rental cost. Budget for this explicitly if it applies, or look for specialist young-driver-friendly suppliers.

This surcharge can be brutal. I've seen cases where a €20 per day rental becomes €45 per day for drivers under 25. Some suppliers in Europe charge up to €30 per day extra for young drivers. If you're under 25, it's worth specifically searching for "young driver" deals — some companies waive or reduce this fee for certain age ranges or during promotional periods.

Cross-Border and Country Restriction Fees

Planning to take your rental from Spain into Portugal, or from Slovenia into Croatia? Many rental contracts prohibit or charge heavily for crossing national borders. Always declare your itinerary upfront and confirm cross-border permission in writing before pickup.

GPS / Child Seat Add-Ons

In-car GPS navigation is largely redundant in 2026 — your phone does it better. Decline it. Child seats are a legal requirement if travelling with young children; book them in advance rather than at the counter, where availability is unreliable and pricing is highest.

Rental companies charge €10-15 per day for GPS units that are often outdated and difficult to use. Your phone's navigation is free, updated in real-time, and generally more accurate. As for child seats, I've seen counter prices of €12 per day for seats that cost €30 to buy outright. If you're travelling with children for more than a few days, it's often cheaper to buy a seat locally and leave it behind or donate it.

Out-of-Hours Pickup Fee

Some suppliers charge extra for pickups outside standard business hours. If your flight lands at 11pm, confirm that the late pickup is either included or explicitly costed before you book — not when you arrive at a closed desk.

I once arrived at a rental counter at 10pm only to find it closed, despite my booking confirmation showing "24-hour pickup." The after-hours service was available but cost an extra €35. Had I known this in advance, I could have either booked a different supplier or adjusted my arrival time. Always confirm after-hours policies before booking.

9. Inspect the Car Thoroughly Before You Drive

This is non-negotiable, every single time. Walk around the car before accepting it, and document every scratch, dent, chip, and scuff — no matter how minor — with time-stamped photos or video from your phone. Do this even if a staff member says "don't worry about small things."

I learned this lesson after being charged for damage I didn't cause. A rental company claimed I scratched a bumper that was already damaged when I picked up the car. Without photographic evidence, I had no way to prove it wasn't me. Now I spend 5 minutes photographing every angle of every rental car, including the interior, tires, and undercarriage if accessible. It's saved me from disputes twice already.

Submit your photographic documentation to the company via email immediately after pickup if they provide any written communication mechanism. This creates a timestamped record that protects you from being billed for damage you didn't cause — a practice that, while not universal, does occur at some suppliers.

Photograph the interior too: check for pre-existing stains, tears, or missing items (charging cables, spare tyre equipment if applicable). Note the fuel level exactly and confirm it matches what the contract states.

Return Documentation

Apply the same photo process on return, ideally with a staff member present to sign off on the condition. If returning out of hours, photograph everything before leaving the key in the drop box, and email the documentation immediately.

10. Consider eSIM for Navigation Without Roaming Charges

Saving money on the rental itself is only part of the picture. One often-overlooked cost during road trips is mobile data — which you need for navigation, offline maps, and communication. International roaming charges from your home carrier can easily add €30–60 to a week-long trip.

For international road trips, pairing your rental with a reliable travel eSIM can significantly reduce roaming costs.

An eSIM from providers like Yesim or Airalo (both via Travelpayouts) offers regional or global data coverage at a fraction of standard roaming rates. You purchase and activate digitally — no physical SIM swap required — and most modern phones support eSIM natively. For a road tripper, this is one of the smartest €10–20 you'll spend.

11. For Transfers: Know When Not to Rent

Sometimes the smartest car rental decision is not to rent at all. For single-city trips, short urban breaks, or itineraries centred on public-transport-rich destinations (Tokyo, Amsterdam, Lisbon centre), a rental car becomes a liability — a parking puzzle, a congestion-charge bill, a stress variable.

For those legs of a trip, private airport transfers and pre-booked taxis through platforms like Kiwitaxi and Welcome Pickups offer door-to-door convenience at transparent fixed rates — no meter anxiety, no surge pricing, no hunting for the car park on arrival.

If you’re visiting a city where driving is unnecessary, you may actually save more by skipping a rental entirely and focusing on experiences instead. Platforms like GetYourGuide make this easy by offering curated tours, day trips, and skip-the-line experiences that often remove the need for a car altogether in major urban destinations.

In cities such as Rome, Barcelona, Paris, or Amsterdam, combining public transport with guided experiences can often be more cost-effective — and significantly less stressful — than renting a car for your entire stay.

Final Thoughts

The cheapest rental car is rarely the one with the lowest advertised daily rate. Real savings come from comparing suppliers carefully, understanding insurance options, avoiding unnecessary extras, and booking early enough to take advantage of price changes.

Whether you're planning a European road trip, an Icelandic adventure, or a family holiday closer to home, these strategies can reduce costs substantially while helping you avoid the most common rental pitfalls.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I book a rental car?

Booking several months ahead with free cancellation typically provides the best combination of availability and price flexibility.

Is airport rental more expensive?

Usually yes. Airport concession fees and higher demand often make airport locations more expensive than city branches.

Should I buy rental company insurance?

Not necessarily. Check whether your credit card or travel insurance already provides rental coverage.

What fuel policy is best?

Full-to-full is generally the most transparent option and gives you the greatest control over costs.

How much can these strategies save?

Many travellers can reduce total rental costs by 20–50% by comparing platforms, avoiding unnecessary insurance, and booking early.
