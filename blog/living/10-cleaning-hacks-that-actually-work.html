10 Cleaning Hacks That Actually Work

Cleaning doesn't need to take over your weekend. With the right techniques — and a few surprisingly effective tricks — you can maintain a clean, pleasant home in far less time than you think.

I've tested a lot of cleaning tips over the years. Some are genuinely clever. Many are nonsense. These ten have earned their place in my regular rotation because they actually save time, actually work, and don't require purchasing seventeen specialised products.

I used to be the person who spent entire weekends cleaning, convinced that more effort meant better results. I'd scrub for hours, use three different products on the same surface, and still feel like I'd barely made a dent. Then I started paying attention to the people whose homes always seemed effortlessly clean — not because they cleaned more, but because they cleaned differently. They worked with systems, not brute force. They used simple ingredients instead of expensive products. They focused on maintenance rather than emergency deep cleans. Over time, I adopted their habits, adapted them to my own life, and discovered that cleaning didn't have to consume my weekends. These ten hacks are the ones that made the biggest difference — not because they're revolutionary, but because they actually work in real life, not just on Pinterest.

1. Clean top to bottom, always

Dust and debris fall downward. If you vacuum first and then dust the shelves, you've just dirtied the floor again. Ceiling to floor, every time.

This sounds obvious, but I spent years doing it backwards without even realizing. I'd vacuum the living room, then dust the bookshelves, then wonder why the floor looked dusty again five minutes later. A friend who worked as a professional house cleaner finally pointed it out to me — she showed me how she starts at the ceiling fans, works down to the shelves, then the tables, and finally the floor. When I tried her method, I was shocked at how much faster the whole process went. Not only did I not have to re-vacuum, but the dusting itself felt more efficient because I wasn't worrying about where the dust was landing. It's a simple change, but it transformed my cleaning routine from frustrating to actually effective.

2. White vinegar is your all-purpose miracle

Diluted 50/50 with water, white vinegar cleans glass, surfaces, taps, and limescale with minimal effort and no harsh chemicals. Keep a spray bottle of it under every sink.

I was skeptical about vinegar for years — it seemed too simple, too cheap, too good to be true. But after a particularly stubborn limescale buildup in my bathroom that nothing else seemed to touch, I finally gave it a try. I sprayed the vinegar solution, let it sit for ten minutes, and wiped it away with a microfiber cloth. The limescale dissolved completely, no scrubbing required. Now I keep vinegar spray bottles in my kitchen, bathroom, and laundry room. It's become my go-to for everything from cleaning windows to wiping down countertops to descaling the kettle. The smell dissipates quickly, and I love that I'm not breathing in harsh chemicals every time I clean my home.

3. Baking soda for the oven

Make a paste of baking soda and water, spread it inside the oven, leave it overnight, and wipe clean in the morning. No chemical oven cleaner smell, no scrubbing. It genuinely works.

I used to dread cleaning my oven — the chemical smell alone was enough to put me off, and the scrubbing seemed endless. A friend suggested the baking soda method, and I admit I was doubtful. How could something so gentle possibly clean baked-on grease? But I tried it anyway, spreading the paste before bed and forgetting about it. The next morning, I wiped it away with a damp cloth, and the grease came off with almost no effort. No harsh fumes, no hours of scrubbing, just a clean oven. I do this once a month now, and my oven stays clean without me ever having to use harsh chemicals again.

4. Use a squeegee on carpet for pet hair

A rubber squeegee dragged across carpet pulls up pet hair that a vacuum leaves behind. Strange to watch, deeply satisfying to see work.

I don't have pets myself, but I housesat for a friend who has two golden retrievers for a week. Her vacuum was decent, but the carpet still looked covered in hair no matter how many times I went over it. I remembered hearing about the squeegee trick and decided to try it out of desperation. I dragged a rubber window squeegee across the carpet, and to my amazement, clumps of hair started rolling up in front of it like magic. It was genuinely satisfying to watch — and even more satisfying to see the carpet actually clean for the first time all week. When my friend returned, she couldn't believe the difference. Now I recommend this trick to anyone with pets, even if they look at me like I'm crazy at first.

5. The "reset" clean vs the deep clean

Distinguish between a daily 10-minute reset (surfaces cleared, dishes away, cushions straight) and a weekly deep clean. Doing the reset daily means the deep clean takes half as long.

This distinction changed my relationship with cleaning entirely. I used to treat every cleaning session as a deep clean — which meant I avoided cleaning because it felt overwhelming. Then I started doing a quick 10-minute reset every evening: clearing surfaces, loading the dishwasher, straightening cushions, wiping down the kitchen counters. It became part of my wind-down routine, like brushing my teeth. When weekend cleaning time came around, my home was already tidy — I only needed to focus on the actual cleaning, not the tidying. What used to take me three hours now takes ninety minutes, and I don't dread it anymore because I'm not starting from chaos.

"Distinguish between a daily 10-minute reset (surfaces cleared, dishes away, cushions straight) and a weekly deep clean. ..."

6. Microfibre cloths over paper towels

Microfibre cloths clean more effectively, leave fewer streaks, and cost far less over time. Keep a stack and wash them weekly.

I used to go through rolls of paper towels like they were going out of style — wiping counters, cleaning mirrors, drying hands, everything. Then a friend gave me a set of microfibre cloths as a housewarming gift, and I admit I was skeptical. How could a cloth possibly replace the convenience of paper towels? But I tried them, and the difference was immediate. They picked up dust and spills that paper towels just smeared around. They left mirrors genuinely streak-free. And when they got dirty, I just tossed them in the wash. A year later, I still have the same set, and I've saved a small fortune on paper towels. I keep a stack in the kitchen, another in the bathroom, and a few in the laundry room for quick cleanups.

7. Descale your kettle with lemon

Fill with water, add a few slices of lemon, boil, let sit, rinse. Limescale dissolves without any specialist product and your kettle smells fresh.

I used to buy expensive descaling solutions for my kettle, convinced that the harsh chemicals were necessary to remove the stubborn limescale buildup. Then I ran out mid-week and decided to try the lemon method as a temporary fix. I filled the kettle with water, added a few lemon slices, boiled it, and let it sit for an hour. When I poured it out and rinsed the kettle, the limescale had dissolved completely — and my kettle smelled amazing, like fresh lemon instead of harsh chemicals. I've never bought descaling solution since. It's cheaper, gentler, and leaves my kitchen smelling wonderful instead of chemical.

8. The two-minute rule for maintenance

If something will take under two minutes to clean or tidy, do it now. The bathroom mirror, the stovetop splatter, the cup ring on the table. Addressing things immediately prevents accumulation.

This rule sounds simple, but it took me months to actually implement it. I used to tell myself "I'll do it later" for every small mess — wipe the counter later, clean the mirror later, put the dish away later. Then "later" would arrive, and I'd be facing a mountain of small tasks that felt overwhelming. A friend suggested the two-minute rule: if it takes less than two minutes, do it now. The first week was hard — I had to constantly remind myself. But then it became automatic. Now I wipe the counter while the coffee brews. I clean the mirror while the water warms up. I put the dish in the dishwasher instead of leaving it in the sink. The result is that my home stays consistently tidy without me ever having to set aside time for "cleaning."

9. Newspaper for streak-free glass

Old-fashioned but real. Balled-up newspaper buffed over glass after cleaning prevents the streaks that even good cloths can leave behind.

My grandmother swore by this trick, and for years I dismissed it as an old wives' tale. Surely modern microfiber cloths were superior to crumpled newspaper? Then one day, after cleaning my windows three times and still seeing streaks, I finally tried her method. I cleaned the glass with my usual solution, then buffed it with balled-up newspaper. The ink didn't transfer, and the streaks disappeared completely. The glass looked professionally clean. I was shocked — and a little annoyed that I'd spent years fighting streaks when the solution was sitting in my recycling bin all along. Now I keep a stack of newspaper specifically for this purpose, and my windows have never looked better.

10. Clean your cleaning tools

A dirty mop, a clogged vacuum filter, or a mouldy spray bottle undoes your cleaning efforts. Monthly maintenance of your tools makes your cleaning exponentially more effective.

This was the hardest lesson for me to learn. I'd spend hours cleaning, only to realize my mop was spreading dirt instead of removing it, or my vacuum wasn't picking up anything because the filter was clogged. A professional cleaner finally pointed out the obvious: you can't clean effectively with dirty tools. Now I set aside one evening a month to maintain my cleaning tools — I wash the mop heads, clean the vacuum filter, run the spray bottles through the dishwasher, and check everything for wear. It takes maybe thirty minutes, but the difference in cleaning effectiveness is enormous. My tools work better, last longer, and I don't end up frustrated by cleaning that doesn't actually clean.

None of these hacks require a complete overhaul of your life or your cleaning routine. The beauty of small, consistent improvements is that they compound over time in ways that sudden big changes never quite manage. Start with one thing — maybe the two-minute rule, or the daily reset. Get comfortable with it. Then add another. Before you know it, cleaning has stopped feeling like a chore and started feeling like a manageable part of your routine.

The people whose homes always seem clean aren't necessarily the most disciplined or the most informed. They're the ones who've stopped treating cleaning as something to get through and started treating it as something to actually enjoy — or at least, something that doesn't drain them. That shift in framing is worth more than any single tip I could give you.
